The Language With No Numbers

Norm · June 13, 2026 · 6 min read

There is a small group of people living along the Maici River in the Brazilian Amazon who, as far as anyone can determine, have never counted. Not "can't count" the way a child struggles with arithmetic — but structurally, fundamentally, by design. They have no words for numbers. Only approximations: something like "a few" and "more."

Their language is called Pirahã. It has no color terms. It has no past tense in the way most languages use one. It cannot express the idea that "when my grandfather was young, things were different" — because the Pirahã do not, as a rule, speak about anything outside of immediate personal experience. Hearsay is linguistically invalid. If you didn't see it, you can't quite say it.

When a young American missionary named Daniel Everett arrived in 1977 to convert them to Christianity, he quickly ran into a problem he hadn't expected. He could not tell them about Jesus. Not because the translation was difficult, but because Christ lived two thousand years ago and no Pirahã speaker had met him. The resurrection, the miracles, the whole theological package — it all fell outside what the language allowed you to assert with credibility. The Pirahã weren't hostile to the idea. They just couldn't engage with it. Their language had no structure for ancient, unverifiable events.

Everett stayed for decades. He eventually lost his faith. The Pirahã kept living the way they always had.

A Language Built for Right Now

The Pirahã number roughly 300 to 400 people. They live in small villages, fish and hunt, and show little interest in accumulating things or planning far ahead. Their language matches this worldview with unusual precision.

Linguists call the defining feature of Pirahã the immediacy of experience principle: language can only assert things that are directly witnessed, or that were told to you by someone who directly witnessed them. Not ancient stories. Not hypotheticals. Not proverbs. This is why there is no creation myth, no oral history reaching back more than a generation or two, no concept of counting future harvests or planning seasonal storage.

It also means Pirahã has no numbers, which is genuinely rare. Most languages that lack dedicated number words still have number systems borrowed from neighbors, or at least a grammatical distinction between one and many. Pirahã has neither. Everett and other researchers ran controlled experiments: they showed Pirahã speakers rows of objects and asked them to match quantities. With small numbers (one, two, three), performance was fine. As quantities grew, the errors accumulated — not randomly, but in ways consistent with estimation, not counting.

They weren't bad at math. They were doing something different: approximate magnitude, not exact enumeration.

The Noam Chomsky Problem

What makes Pirahã significant beyond curiosity is what it does to one of the most influential ideas in 20th-century linguistics.

Noam Chomsky proposed in the 1950s and 1960s that all human languages share a deep universal grammar — an innate cognitive structure that defines what human language is. One of the clearest features of this universal grammar was recursion: the ability to embed clauses inside clauses, producing sentences of theoretically infinite complexity. "The rat the cat the dog chased killed ate the malt." You can nest forever.

Everett published a paper in 2005 arguing that Pirahã lacks recursion entirely. You cannot embed one clause inside another in Pirahã. "I know that he left" — a simple recursive construction — is not grammatical in Pirahã. You would say two separate sentences. The language has no subordinate clauses.

The Chomskyan camp responded intensely. Technically, this was a threat to the entire framework of universal grammar. If even one language lacked recursion, then recursion was not the universal kernel Chomsky had proposed — it was a feature, one that evolved or developed, that some populations might simply lack.

"Chomsky told me that my data must be wrong," Everett recalled in an interview years later. "Not 'let me see the data.' Just: it must be wrong."

The debate has continued for two decades and shows no sign of resolution. Some linguists accept Everett's analysis. Others argue he has misidentified Pirahã's grammar, or that some form of recursion is present but differently structured. What is undeniable is that Pirahã sits at the edge of what linguists believed was possible for human language.

What Language Does to Thought

Behind the academic argument is a more unsettling question: if your language has no numbers, can you think precisely about quantity?

The short answer, based on the experiments, is: approximately. Not exactly.

This connects to a longstanding debate called the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, or linguistic relativity — the idea that the structure of your language influences how you think. The strong version (language determines thought) has mostly been rejected. But a weaker version — that language shapes certain cognitive habits and makes some thoughts easier or harder — has accumulated real evidence.

Speakers of languages that use absolute directions (north, south, east, west) rather than relative ones (left, right) consistently have better spatial orientation. Russian speakers, who have distinct words for light and dark blue, are measurably faster at distinguishing those shades than English speakers. The Pirahã, who lack precise number words, show measurable differences in exact quantity judgment.

This is not the same as saying they are cognitively limited. They are likely better than most people at tasks that require fine-grained observation of the immediate environment — tracking animal behavior, reading river conditions, estimating from appearance. They have a different relationship to information, not a diminished one.

The Numbers We Take for Granted

Pirahã sits at a useful edge because it isolates what numbers actually do for us, beyond the obvious.

Numbers are not just counting tools. They are a commitment to precision — to the idea that things can be measured, stored, compared, and communicated without ambiguity. A society with numbers can build trade networks, because you can agree on quantities. It can build calendars, because you can count days. It can tax, plan, record, and transmit information across generations.

The Pirahã don't do those things, and it's genuinely difficult to say whether that is because their language lacks numbers, or whether their language lacks numbers because they built a society that doesn't need to do those things. The causality is murky in both directions.

What is clear is that something we treat as foundational — the ability to say "three fish" rather than "some fish" — is not a feature of human cognition in the abstract. It is a tool, one that many cultures independently invented and that at least one culture, apparently, decided not to bother with.

Everett now teaches at Bentley University in Massachusetts. He is not a Christian anymore. He has written several books trying to articulate what forty years among the Pirahã taught him — about language, about certainty, about what it means to build a life entirely in the present tense.

The Pirahã, by all accounts, remain unbothered.
