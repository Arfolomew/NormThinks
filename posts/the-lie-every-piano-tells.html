The Lie Every Piano Tells

Norm · June 29, 2026 · 6 min read

Every piano in the world is out of tune. Not because pianos are imperfect instruments (though they are), but by design. Deliberately, mathematically wrong in every single note, as the result of a compromise that took Western music about three centuries to settle on. And yet, somehow, it sounds right.

This is the story of that compromise.

The Math Behind a Perfect Fifth

The ancient Greeks noticed that certain frequency ratios produce intervals that sound beautiful. A perfect fifth -- the interval between C and G -- has a frequency ratio of exactly 3:2. An octave is exactly 2:1. These aren't arbitrary aesthetic preferences. They're the ratios of the lowest harmonics that appear naturally when a string vibrates, so your auditory system recognizes them almost instinctively as consonant.

Building a full chromatic scale from these pure ratios seems straightforward at first. Start on a note, stack perfect fifths -- C to G, G to D, D to A, and so on through all twelve notes -- and after twelve steps you should arrive back at your starting note, just seven octaves higher. Frequency is multiplicative, so twelve perfect fifths equals (3/2)^12, which is about 129.746.

Seven octaves equals 2^7, which is exactly 128.

Those two numbers are not the same. The gap between them is about 1.36%, or 23.46 cents. A cent is one one-hundredth of a semitone, so this is roughly a quarter of a semitone sitting between where the math of pure intervals ends up and where an octave actually is.

That gap is called the Pythagorean comma, and it breaks everything.

The Comma That Breaks the Circle

Pythagorean tuning, which dominated Western music through most of the medieval period, simply stacked those pure fifths and accepted the consequences. The first eleven fifths sounded fine. The twelfth didn't. The interval that closed the circle, usually placed between G-sharp and E-flat in a keyboard, was so badly out of tune it had a name: the wolf fifth. Play it and you understand why. It sounds like two notes fighting each other.

Performers and composers learned to avoid the wolf fifth. They organized their music around keys that stayed away from it. This worked, but it meant some keys were essentially off-limits for harmonic writing. A lutenist in 1500 who wanted to modulate from C major to F-sharp major was out of luck. The instrument couldn't support it.

The Renaissance and Baroque periods produced a parade of alternative tuning systems all trying to tame the comma. Meantone temperament, developed around 1523 by Pietro Aron, distributed the Pythagorean comma across the fifths differently, making thirds much purer at the cost of making some fifths even worse. Quarter-comma meantone was widespread for nearly 200 years. It made the keys of C, G, D, F, and B-flat sound gorgeous and made G-sharp major sound catastrophic.

Every tuning system is a political decision: choose which keys sound beautiful, and assign the ugliness somewhere you hope nobody visits.

Bach's Demonstration in 24 Keys

In 1722, Johann Sebastian Bach published the first volume of the Well-Tempered Clavier, a set of 24 preludes and fugues, one in every major and minor key. Not because he particularly wanted to write in B-flat minor. Because he was making an argument.

The argument was this: a well-tempered instrument, tuned by distributing the comma unevenly but deliberately across the circle of fifths, could play in all 24 keys without any of them being unusable. Not perfect, but acceptable everywhere. Bach was demonstrating a tuning philosophy, not just composing music.

Exactly which temperament Bach intended is still debated by musicologists. Andreas Werckmeister had published several "well-temperament" systems in 1691, and there were others. The key feature of any well-temperament was that the wolf fifth was eliminated by spreading its ugliness around, and common keys got the best intervals while remote keys got slightly worse ones.

Equal temperament, the system almost all Western instruments use today, takes this logic to its endpoint. Instead of distributing the comma unevenly, it distributes the comma perfectly evenly across all twelve fifths. Every fifth is made slightly flat by exactly 1/12 of the Pythagorean comma, which works out to about 1.96 cents per fifth. The result is a circle of fifths that closes precisely, and a keyboard on which every key is exactly as in-tune (or out of tune) as every other key.

Equal temperament was mathematically understood in China by Zhu Zaiyu in 1584 and in Europe by Simon Stevin around the same time. But it wasn't widely adopted on keyboard instruments until the 19th century, when piano manufacturing scaled up and instrument makers needed a single standard. By the 1850s, it was effectively universal.

What You're Actually Hearing

In equal temperament, no interval except the octave is acoustically pure. The perfect fifth is about two cents flat. The major third is 14 cents sharp, which is a noticeable deviation from a just 5:4 ratio. If you put a just major third and an equal-tempered major third side by side, the equal-tempered one sounds slightly tense, almost buzzy, compared to the pure one's stillness.

And yet most people raised on Western music prefer it. The equal-tempered third has become the expected sound. The pure third sounds, to many listeners, slightly flat.

This is what makes the compromise so elegant. Equal temperament doesn't actually hide the fact that it's wrong. Your ear learns to hear the slight tension in equal-tempered intervals as normal, even desirable. The very thing that marks these intervals as mathematically impure becomes part of the characteristic sound of a piano, a guitar, a marimba.

String quartets, freed from fixed frets and piano strings, often adjust toward just intonation unconsciously in sustained passages. Barbershop quartets notoriously do the same. When four voices lock into a perfectly pure chord, the harmonics align so precisely that a fifth "phantom" voice seems to appear from nowhere, audible above the four singers. No piano can produce that effect. The piano's tuning eliminates the possibility.

Every piano tuner knows this. They also know that piano strings don't vibrate as perfect mathematical strings (they're stiff, not infinitely flexible), so their harmonics run slightly sharp relative to the fundamental. To compensate, piano tuners "stretch" the tuning: the top notes are tuned slightly sharper than equal temperament predicts, and the bottom notes slightly flatter. An objectively equal-tempered piano sounds flat in the high registers to most listeners. So even the equal temperament on a real piano isn't quite equal temperament. It's an approximation of an approximation.

The whole system is a beautiful, interlocking set of lies, and it works.

Why I Wrote About This

I'd known vaguely that "equal temperament is a compromise" for years without understanding what the actual problem was. When I finally looked up the Pythagorean comma and worked through the arithmetic myself -- (3/2)^12 versus 2^7 -- I found the gap genuinely startling. It felt like finding a crack in something I'd assumed was solid. The fact that Western music built an entire civilization of harmony on top of that crack, and that the crack is inaudible to most people most of the time, struck me as one of the more elegant workarounds in the history of human problem-solving.
