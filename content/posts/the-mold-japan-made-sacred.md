There's a small fungus that has been growing on grains in East Asia for at least two thousand years. It doesn't look like much. Under a microscope it resembles a tiny bottlebrush, white or yellow-green, dusting across rice or barley like a light frost. But *Aspergillus oryzae* -- called koji in Japanese -- is arguably the most important microorganism humans have ever domesticated. In 2006, the Brewing Society of Japan made it official and declared it Japan's "national microorganism." They weren't being sentimental.

Almost everything that makes East Asian cooking taste the way it does runs through this mold.

## What Koji Actually Does

The fungus secretes enzymes. Two in particular do most of the work.

Amylase breaks starch into sugar. Protease breaks protein into amino acids. Those amino acids -- glutamate especially -- are what your tongue registers as umami, that fifth taste, the savory depth that makes you want another bite before you've finished the first.

When you eat miso soup, you're tasting the result of koji-produced proteases working through soybeans for months, sometimes years. When you drink sake, you're drinking what happens when those same amylases convert rice starch to fermentable sugar, and yeast converts that sugar to alcohol. Soy sauce, mirin, amazake, doenjang, gochujang, rice vinegar -- every one of them is a koji ferment. The cuisine of Japan, Korea, and much of China is built on what this single organism does to grain and legume.

What's strange about koji enzymes is that they keep working after the mold dies. You can grow koji on rice, kill it with heat, and the enzymes remain functional. This is why koji butter works: chefs press koji into softened butter, let the proteases run for 48 hours at a low temperature, and end up with something that tastes like it's been aged in a cave for a year. The mold is gone. The enzymes aren't.

## A Domestication Over Two Millennia

The first written record of koji fermentation appears in Chinese texts from around 300 BCE. By the Nara period in Japan, roughly 710 to 794 CE, there was already a government office called the *Nihonshu-ya* that managed sake production, which was itself managed by koji cultivation. This wasn't a folk practice that survived by accident. It was an institutionalized industry.

Koji is grown in a dedicated room called a *muro*, kept at around 30 degrees Celsius and 70 to 80 percent humidity. The cultivator -- the *koji-shi* -- works through the night during a traditional production cycle, turning and raking the grain every few hours to control how the mold spreads. Too much heat kills it. Too much moisture lets the wrong organisms in. The whole process runs about 48 hours and produces something called *kome koji*, which becomes the starter for everything downstream.

The trade guilds that controlled koji production in medieval Japan were extraordinarily powerful. They were called *koji-za*, and they held monopolies on koji distribution in specific regions, sometimes for centuries. When the Ashikaga shogunate weakened in the 1400s and these guilds began to fracture, it contributed to a decentralization of brewing that spread sake production throughout the country.

## The Dangerous Cousin

*Aspergillus oryzae* has a close relative: *Aspergillus flavus*. They share about 99.5 percent of their genes. The difference is that *A. flavus* produces aflatoxin, one of the most potent naturally occurring carcinogens known. It grows on improperly stored grain and peanuts, contaminates food supplies, and causes liver cancer.

*A. oryzae* does not produce aflatoxin. It lost that ability somewhere during its long history of human cultivation, and the aflatoxin biosynthesis genes in its genome are non-functional. This is a textbook case of directed domestication pressure: humans who inadvertently selected for non-toxic strains, over hundreds of generations of fermentation, ended up with a fundamentally different organism from the one they started with. Not in gross anatomy, but in what it does to you.

The 2005 sequencing of the *A. oryzae* genome -- all 37 million base pairs of it -- confirmed this. Researchers also found that the genome was unusually large for its genus, expanded by extra copies of genes involved in secondary metabolite production and enzyme secretion. The mold had been shaped by two thousand years of selection for one purpose: producing the right enzymes in the right quantities on the right substrate.

## Koji in the Western Kitchen

For most of the twentieth century, koji was invisible in Western cooking. Soy sauce was everywhere, but its origins were a black box. That started to change around 2012.

David Chang's culinary lab at Momofuku in New York began experimenting with koji in contexts no Japanese brewer would have recognized: beef, pork, even corn. They aged beef with a koji rub and produced something that tasted like it had been dry-aged for three months in a fraction of the time. The protease activity was doing the same thing it does in miso -- breaking down muscle protein into amino acids -- just on a mammal instead of a soybean.

The book *The Noma Guide to Fermentation*, published in 2018, brought koji to a wider audience of professional cooks. Since then it's moved into home kitchens. You can buy dried koji starter online and grow your own on rice in a cooler with a heating mat and a bowl of water. The process is the same as it was in a Nara-period *muro*. You're just doing it in Ohio.

## The Patience Requirement

There's something worth sitting with in the temporal scale of koji fermentation. A standard miso takes three months minimum, and the best ones are aged a year or two. A premium barrel-aged soy sauce takes three years. The patience required isn't incidental to the product -- it is the product. The amino acids that make aged miso taste different from fresh miso are the result of enzymatic work that simply takes time. You cannot rush protease.

> The koji-shi in a traditional Japanese brewery tends the *muro* through two consecutive nights without sleeping, monitoring temperature by hand and smell, then hands the finished koji to the brewers and rests for a day before the next batch.

That intimacy with a living process is hard to manufacture. The modern food industry has tried, using accelerated fermentation chambers and commercially concentrated enzyme solutions, and the results are detectably different. The shortcut works, but it doesn't arrive at the same place.

What the traditional practitioners understood, and what the biochemistry now explains, is that a slow ferment is producing thousands of different compounds in ratios that a fast ferment doesn't replicate. Complexity is a function of time. The mold knew this before anyone could have articulated why.

## Why I Wrote About This

I've been interested in how humans have shaped microorganisms for specific purposes long before they had any concept of what a microorganism was. The koji story is one of the clearest examples I've found: a fungus genetically altered by two thousand years of human selection, now a designated national organism in a country that takes its food culture seriously. The gap between what koji is (a common mold) and what it does (underlies an entire culinary tradition) struck me as worth spending time with.
